## The Revenue Miss You're Dismissing Is Actually a Warning Signal

**This is the second consecutive quarter of decelerating top-line growth.**

And what's driving that? You mentioned legacy game revenue compressing. But games were historically BILI's *core identity.* The fact that the legacy business is shrinking faster than advertising can compensate tells me this company is still figuring out its long-term revenue mix.

When a "growth company" delivers single-digit revenue growth and trades at 45x earnings, that's not a value play. That's **paying a premium for a company that's losing its growth premium.**

## The Profitability Story Is Real—But Misleading

Let me give you credit where it's due: moving from -38% operating margins to +3.7% is genuinely impressive. But here's what you're glossing over:

**$5.32 billion in free cash flow sounds incredible until you ask: what drove it?**

Look at the cash flow statement more carefully. The massive swing from -6.65B in FCF in 2022 to +5.32B in 2025 includes **one-time working capital benefits and aggressive cost-cutting that isn't sustainable at the same rate going forward.**

Research and development spending dropped from 21.8% of revenue in 2022 to 11.6% in 2025. Selling and marketing went from 22.5% to 14.5%. Those are *structural cuts*—and they're great for margins. But at what cost to future growth?

A company that reinvests less in R&D and marketing is a company that's *harvesting its existing user base, not planting seeds for the next wave of users.* That works for a few quarters. Then growth decelerates further. And then the multiple compresses.

You're celebrating the *result* of cost-cutting while ignoring that it's a finite resource. You can't cut your way to sustainable growth.

## The Net Cash Argument Is Overstated

This is your strongest point, and I want to address it directly. Yes, BILI has roughly 24.2B CNY in cash against 9.6B in debt, giving net cash of ~14.6B CNY.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: **that cash is held in China.**

For a NASDAQ-listed ADR, Chinese cash doesn't provide the same "margin of safety" that it would for a US-based company. Capital controls, regulatory restrictions, and the risk that Beijing could restrict cash outflows mean that US ADR holders can't necessarily access that cash for buybacks or dividends on their terms.

The fact that BILI *did* do share buybacks (822.7M CNY in 2025) is positive, but the sheer size of the cash pile relative to the market cap also raises another question: **why isn't management being more aggressive with it?**

If the stock is truly 38.5% undervalued at $17.58, why aren't they buying back *more*? Why is the cash just sitting there? Either management doesn't agree with the undervaluation thesis, or they're constrained by regulatory realities that you're not factoring in.
